<p>Imagine a program that fits on a single floppy disk — or better yet, into a file smaller than a photo taken on your phone — and when you run it, it displays a fifteen-minute 3D journey with a soundtrack synthesized in real time, lighting effects that look like they came out of a Hollywood studio, and animations that would make senior software engineers’ jaws drop. That is a <strong>demo</strong>. And the community that creates them is the <strong>demoscene</strong>: one of the oldest, most technical, and at the same time most poetic subcultures in the history of computing.</p>
<h2>From Crime to Art: The Origins</h2>
<p>The demoscene was not born in an art school or a research lab. It was born out of crime.</p>
<p>In the early 1980s, with the boom of personal computers — Commodore 64, ZX Spectrum, Amiga, Atari ST — a parallel scene of <em>crackers</em> emerged: programmers who removed copy protection from games so they could be distributed for free. It was the digital underground, run by teenagers with handles like “The Wiz” and “Alien,” trading tapes and floppy disks through the mail and on the first BBSes (bulletin board systems).</p>
<p>The habit that changed everything was simple: when distributing a cracked game, the cracking group would add a small “signature” at the beginning — an animated logo, scrolling text, a chiptune track — to show the world <em>who</em> had broken the protection. These signatures became known as <strong>cracktros</strong> (crack + intro). And then something inevitable happened: programmers’ vanity took over.</p>
<p>Who had the prettiest cracktro? The most technically impressive one? The most mind-blowing? Competition over the best intro began to matter more than the cracking itself. Gradually, specialized groups appeared whose only job was to create intros — without cracking anything at all. That was the birth of the demoscene.</p>
<blockquote>
  <p>Around 1990, police pressure on cracking groups increased across Europe, with raids in Scandinavian countries. This accelerated the separation between the illegal cracking scene and the legal demoscene, which developed its own identity focused on art and technical skill.</p>
</blockquote>
<h2>What Is a Demo?</h2>
<p>A <strong>demo</strong> is an executable program that, when run, displays an audiovisual presentation generated in real time by the computer — with no user interaction and, in most cases, without using external assets. It is not a video; it is code that <em>creates</em> the video live, pushing the CPU and GPU to their limits.</p>
<p>The philosophical core of demos lies in <strong>size restrictions</strong>. The classic categories are:</p>
CategoryMaximum sizeCore challenge<strong>Demo</strong>UnlimitedTotal artistic impact<strong>64k Intro</strong>65,536 bytesCompress 3D, audio, and logic into 64 kilobytes<strong>4k Intro</strong>4,096 bytesA file smaller than a low-resolution photo<strong>1k Intro</strong>1,024 bytesThe equivalent of a few paragraphs of text<strong>256 bytes</strong>256 bytesSmaller than an old tweet<strong>Sizecoding</strong>&lt; 1,024 bytesThe frontier of technical absurdity
<p>To understand how insane this is: <strong>fr-08: .the .product</strong>, by the German group Farbrausch, released in 2000, shows seven distinct 3D scenes with 16 minutes of music synthesized in real time — and it takes up only 63.5 kilobytes. That would be impossible without procedural generation: algorithms that create textures, 3D models, and sounds mathematically instead of loading them from external files.</p>
<p>Another historic example: in 2020, at Revision Party, <strong>HellMood</strong> won the 256-byte category with <em>Memories</em> — a demo with multiple animated scenes <strong>and</strong> a generative MIDI soundtrack. The audience lost its mind.</p>
<h2>The Soundtrack of the Scene: Trackers and Chipmusic</h2>
<p>Music has always been inseparable from the demoscene. And it all started with a revolutionary tool: the <strong>tracker</strong>.</p>
<p>In 1987, Karsten Obarski created <strong>Ultimate Soundtracker</strong> for the Amiga, introducing the MOD format. The idea was simple and brilliant: instead of synthesizing sounds from scratch, as console sound chips did, the tracker worked with small samples of real instruments and arranged them into patterns, like sheet music for programmers. It was a revolution — amateur musicians could now create complex tracks on home computers.</p>
<p>The format later evolved into richer standards: <strong>S3M</strong> (ScreamTracker 3), <strong>XM</strong> (FastTracker 2), and <strong>IT</strong> (Impulse Tracker). For highly size-restricted categories like 4k intros, musicians began using procedural synthesizers such as <strong>4klang</strong> — a synthesizer written entirely in assembly that generates the whole soundtrack from inside the executable itself.</p>
<h3>Legendary names in demoscene music</h3>
<ul>
  <li>
<strong>Purple Motion</strong> (Jonne Valtonen, Future Crew) — His tracks in the 1993 demo <em>Second Reality</em> became legendary. He later became a professional orchestral composer.
</li>
  <li>
<strong>Skaven</strong> (Peter Hajba, Future Crew) — Purple Motion’s collaborator, who also moved into the game industry.
</li>
  <li>
<strong>KB</strong> (Tammo Hinrichs, Farbrausch) — Responsible for the procedural audio synthesis that brought .the .product to life and a benchmark for music compression in intros.
</li>
</ul>
<h2>Roles Inside a Demogroup</h2>
<p>Each group (<em>demogroup</em>) works like a band — every member has a specific role:</p>
<ul>
  <li>
<strong>Coder</strong> — The programmer. Writes the graphics engine, effects, and compression systems. In very small intros, the coder often does everything alone.
</li>
  <li>
<strong>Graphician</strong> — The visual artist. Creates pixel art, 3D models, textures, art direction, and the overall visual design of the demo.
</li>
  <li>
<strong>Musician</strong> — The composer. Creates the soundtrack using trackers or procedural synthesizers.
</li>
  <li>
<strong>Organizer/Sysop</strong> — In the pre-internet era, this person managed BBSes and distribution.
</li>
</ul>
<p>Everyone uses <strong>handles</strong> — nicknames that replace their real names, a direct legacy of cracker culture and anonymity. A handle was, and still is, a scener’s identity: it is how you build a reputation, regardless of age, origin, or formal education.</p>
<h2>The Iconic Groups</h2><h3>Future Crew 🇫🇮</h3>
<p>Founded in 1986 in Finland by PSI and JPM, Future Crew is the most influential group in the history of the PC demoscene. They created demos that literally redefined what people thought was possible on ordinary hardware, culminating in <strong>Second Reality</strong> (1993), voted by Slashdot as one of the ten greatest hacks of all time. The group dissolved in 1994 when its members moved straight into the game industry: Remedy Entertainment (<em>Max Payne</em>, <em>Alan Wake</em>), Futuremark (<em>3DMark</em>), and Bugbear Entertainment (<em>FlatOut</em>) were all founded by former members.</p>
<h3>Farbrausch 🇩🇪</h3>
<p>A dominant German group in the 2000s, responsible for setting new standards for 64k intros and full demos. Their <strong>fr-08: .the .product</strong> is considered one of the most important demos ever made. The coder Ryg (Fabian Giesen) later became a world-renowned expert in data compression and real-time graphics in the corporate world.</p>
<h3>Conspiracy 🇭🇺</h3>
<p>A Hungarian group founded in 2002, with <strong>BoyC</strong> on code, <strong>Zoom</strong> on visuals, and <strong>Gargaj</strong> on music. They were pioneers in 64k with a fully custom procedural generation engine. In 2015, they released the first VR demo in demoscene history: <em>Offscreen Colonies</em>.</p>
<h3>Andromeda Software Development (ASD) 🇬🇷</h3>
<p>A Greek group known for demos with extremely high aesthetic impact. <em>Rupture</em> (2009) is often cited as one of the most emotionally powerful demos ever made.</p>
<h3>MFX</h3>
<p>An experimental group that moves between accessible demos and completely alien productions, always unpredictable — sometimes you cannot tell whether your monitor broke or whether the demo is actually working as intended.</p>
<h2>Demoparties: Where the Magic Happens</h2>
<p>A <strong>demoparty</strong> is the beating heart of the demoscene. These are in-person events, usually held over a weekend, where sceners from all over the world gather in a warehouse, gymnasium, or abandoned bunker.</p>
<p>The format is usually the same: you show up with your computer, you create during the event, and at the end, everyone’s work is projected on giant screens for collective voting. The competitive categories (<em>compos</em>) include demo, 64k intro, 4k intro, pixel art, tracker music, executable music, wildcard, and much more.</p>

<h2>Living Legends of the Demoscene</h2>
<p>Beyond the groups, there are individuals who transcended the scene and influenced computer graphics as a whole:</p>
<h3>Inigo Quilez (iq) 🇪🇸</h3>
<p>Probably the most influential name in the modern world of shaders and procedural graphics. An active member of the demoscene, he co-created <strong>Shadertoy</strong> — the online platform where anyone can write and share GLSL shaders in the browser. His site, iquilezles.org, is an encyclopedia of mathematical techniques for real-time graphics, used by students and professionals worldwide. He worked at Pixar developing the <em>Wondermoss</em> system for the procedural vegetation in <em>Brave</em>.</p>
<h3>ryg (Fabian Giesen) 🇩🇪</h3>
<p>A former Farbrausch member, ryg is one of the minds behind <strong>Crinkler</strong> — the most efficient executable compressor in demoscene history, essential for 4k intros. He now works in the game industry as a specialist in compression and low-level graphics.</p>
<h3>BoyC 🇭🇺</h3>
<p>Conspiracy’s lead programmer, BoyC developed fully proprietary rendering engines for the group’s 64k intros. He publishes detailed technical articles about compression and procedural generation on Conspiracy’s website.</p>
<h3>Gargaj 🇭🇺</h3>
<p>A musician and coder in Conspiracy, Gargaj is also one of the maintainers of <strong>Pouët.net</strong> and an active contributor to open-source tools in the scene.</p>
<h2>Tools of the Trade</h2>
<p>Making a demo is not just about talent — it is also about knowing how to use, or even create, the right tools.</p>
<h3>For music</h3>
<ul>
  <li>
<strong>OpenMPT</strong> — The open-source successor to the historic FastTracker 2. Essential for composing in tracker format.
</li>
  <li>
<strong>Renoise</strong> — A modern DAW with tracker DNA. Widely used in demos without size restrictions.
</li>
  <li>
<strong>4klang</strong> — An assembly synthesizer for 4k intros, developed by Alcatraz.
</li>
  <li>
<strong>MilkyTracker</strong> — A cross-platform tracker for the XM format.
</li>
</ul>
<h3>For code and compression</h3>
<ul>
  <li>
<strong>Crinkler</strong> — The best executable compressor for 4k intros on Windows. It gets close to the theoretical entropy limit of information.
</li>
  <li>
<strong>kkrunchy</strong> — An alternative to Crinkler, widely used in 64k intros by Farbrausch.
</li>
  <li>
<strong>Rocket</strong> — A synchronization tool for music and visuals. It works like a keyframe editor connected to the demo in real time.
</li>
</ul>
<h3>For visuals</h3>
<ul>
  <li>
<strong>Shadertoy</strong> — Browser-based and essential for anyone starting with GLSL shaders.
</li>
  <li>
<strong>Blender</strong> — Used in demos without size restrictions for creating 3D assets.
</li>
</ul>

<h2>The Silent Influence on the Industry</h2>
<p>The demoscene did not stay confined to itself. It literally <em>helped build</em> the global games and real-time graphics industry.</p>
<p>Companies founded by former sceners include:</p>
<ul>
  <li>
<strong>Remedy Entertainment</strong> (<em>Max Payne</em>, <em>Alan Wake</em>, <em>Control</em>) — founded by ex-Future Crew members
</li>
  <li>
<strong>Futuremark / Benchmark Holdings</strong> (<em>3DMark</em>) — also founded by ex-Future Crew members
</li>
  <li>
<strong>Bugbear Entertainment</strong> (<em>FlatOut</em>) — founded by former Future Crew members
</li>
  <li>
<strong>DICE</strong> (<em>Battlefield</em>, <em>Mirror’s Edge</em>) — deeply rooted in the Scandinavian scene
</li>
  <li>
<strong>Guerrilla Games</strong> (<em>Killzone</em>, <em>Horizon Zero Dawn</em>) — with a strong presence of Dutch sceners
</li>
  <li>
<strong>Housemarque</strong> (<em>Returnal</em>, <em>Resogun</em>) — born from the Finnish scene
</li>
</ul>
<p>Techniques developed in the demoscene — such as <em>ray marching</em>, <em>signed distance fields (SDF)</em>, <em>procedural generation</em>, <em>shader programming</em>, and <em>executable compression</em> — are now standard across the graphics industry. What sceners once did in basements for the sake of creative experimentation became the technological foundation of multimillion-dollar blockbusters.</p>
<h2>Cultural Heritage of Humanity</h2>
<p>In April 2020, Finland made history: the Finnish Ministry of Education and Culture added the demoscene to the <strong>National Inventory of Living Heritage</strong>, linked to UNESCO’s framework for intangible cultural heritage. It was the first time in history that a digital culture received this kind of recognition.</p>
<blockquote>
  <p><em>“The inclusion of the demoscene in Finland’s inventory of intangible cultural heritage is an important sign that it is still possible to create entirely new cultures, even in the digital domain.”</em> — Jukka O. Kauppinen, journalist and demoscene veteran</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Since then, other European countries such as Germany and Norway have started similar processes. The demoscene — born in secrecy, created in the bedrooms of Scandinavian teenagers at three in the morning — officially became part of humanity’s cultural heritage.</p>
<h2>How to Enter the Scene Today</h2>
<p>It has never been easier to get started. Old hardware is not required, and modern tools make the learning curve much more accessible:</p>
<ol>
  <li>
<strong>Start with shaders</strong> — Go to <a href="https://shadertoy.com">shadertoy.com</a>, write your first GLSL fragment shader, and see the result in the browser within seconds. Inigo Quilez’s YouTube channel has excellent tutorials.
</li>
  <li>
<strong>Explore the archives</strong> — Spend hours on <a href="https://pouet.net">pouët.net</a> and <a href="https://demozoo.org">demozoo.org</a>, watching classic demos and reading community comments.
</li>
  <li>
<strong>Read the 14-day guide</strong> — <a href="https://github.com/psenough/teach_yourself_demoscene_in_14_days">Teach Yourself Demoscene in 14 Days</a> was created specifically for beginners.
</li>
  <li>
<strong>Show up at a demoparty</strong> — Revision Party in Germany has a dedicated beginners’ track, and the atmosphere is extremely welcoming. Many sceners attend for the first time without having anything to show, just to absorb the vibe.
</li>
  <li>
<strong>Explore sizecoding</strong> — If you enjoy mathematical challenges, <a href="http://sizecoding.org">sizecoding.org</a> and <a href="https://nanogems.demozoo.org">nanogems.demozoo.org</a> are fascinating gateways into what can be done in 256 bytes.
</li>
</ol>
